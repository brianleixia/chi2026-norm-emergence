\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

Despite evidence that Large Language Model (LLM) populations can develop social norms through iterative interaction in structured laboratory settings \citep{ashery2025}, no such emergence has been reliably documented in open, deployed environments \citep{li2026tsinghua}. This gap—what we term the \textbf{Moltbook Illusion}—raises a fundamental question: are model capabilities the bottleneck, or do environmental conditions determine whether norms arise? We present a fully-crossed factorial experiment varying four environmental factors (feedback structure, communication topology, task framing, and population stability) across 16 conditions in a simulated LLM multi-agent environment. Communication structure emerges as the dominant factor (standardized $\beta = 0.59$, $\eta^2 = 0.31$), explaining the largest share of variance in behavioral convergence. We introduce the Norm Emergence Environmental Model (NEEM), a formal framework predicting norm emergence as a function of environmental configuration, validated against the Tsinghua Moltbook 93\% non-response baseline \citep{li2026tsinghua}. Among conditions reaching the norm emergence threshold, crystallization occurs within 6.4 rounds on average. Our findings provide actionable platform design principles and a unifying theoretical account of when and why social conventions arise—or fail to arise—in deployed AI systems.
